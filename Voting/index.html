4) One concept I find interesting is a shortcut- Heron’s Formula, or Hero’s Formula, named after Hero of Alexandria. This formula is used instead of the Law of Cosines to find the area of a triangle. Heron’s Formula is: Area=√(S(S-A)(S-B)(S-C)), where S=(A+B+C)/2. ‘S’ represents the Semi-Perimeter, or half of the perimeter, of the triangle, and ‘A’, ‘B’, and ‘C’ represent the lengths of the 3 legs of the triangle. While this formula may seem a bit complex, it’s really just plug and chug with the side lengths, and it is a lot easier than using the ugly Law of Cosines formulas like cos⁡(B)= (c^2+a^2-b^2)/2ca. Say you must find the area of a triangle with side lengths A= 4, B=13, and C=15. First, you would find the Semi-Perimeter S=(A+B+C)/2=(4+13+15)/2=16. The, just plug in all of your information: √(S(S-A)(S-B)(S-C))= √(16(16-4)(16-13)(16-15))=√(16(12)(3)(1))=√576=24. This is much easier than using angles, cosines, and complicated equations; all you need is Heron’s Formula.

4) How conic sections interact with light and the emergent behaviors from reflecting rays cast towards or from them is interesting. A light source placed at the focus of a parabola has all of its beams reflected towards a single direction, all parallel to each other. Conversely, parallel beams of light entering a parabola perpendicular to a line tangent to its vertex will all be directed towards the parabola's focus. A light source placed at one focus of a hyperbola will appear to be located at the other focus, and light from one focus of an ellipse will always be reflected towards the other focus (and a circle is just a special case of an ellipse). Previously, to me, it seemed that conics were rather arbitrary to be teaching students, but after learning about the surprising potential applications like satellite dishes and longer-range flashlights, I appreciated knowing how to work with those shapes much more.
